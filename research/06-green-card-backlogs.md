# Green Card Priority Date Backlogs by Country of Birth

*Research date: April 7, 2026 | Data source: April 2026 Visa Bulletin (FY2026)*

---

## 1. How the Visa Bulletin Works

The U.S. Department of State publishes a monthly [Visa Bulletin](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/legal/visa-law0/visa-bulletin.html) that controls when employment-based (EB) green card applicants can file and when their cases can be approved. The bulletin contains two charts:

- **Final Action Dates (FAD):** The cutoff date by which your priority date must fall for your green card to actually be *approved* and permanent residence granted.
- **Dates for Filing (DFF):** An earlier cutoff that lets you *file* your I-485 adjustment of status application before your Final Action Date is current. Filing early lets you get an EAD work permit and advance parole while waiting.

Each month, [USCIS announces](https://www.uscis.gov/green-card/green-card-processes-and-procedures/visa-availability-priority-dates/adjustment-of-status-filing-charts-from-the-visa-bulletin) which chart applicants should use. For April 2026, USCIS directed all employment-based categories to use the Dates for Filing chart.

**Key terms:**
- **Priority Date:** The date your PERM labor certification was filed (EB-2/EB-3) or your I-140 petition was filed (EB-1, NIW). This is your place in line.
- **Current:** No backlog -- any approved petition can proceed immediately.
- **Country of Chargeability:** Determined by your *country of birth*, not citizenship or residence. This is what determines which column of the bulletin applies to you.

## 3. Backlog by Country: The Three-Tier Reality

### India: The Deepest Backlog

- **EB-2 India FAD:** July 15, 2014 -- a **12+ year** backlog
- **EB-3 India FAD:** November 15, 2013 -- a **12.5+ year** backlog
- **EB-1 India FAD:** December 1, 2023 -- a **~2.5 year** backlog
- Approximately **1.1 million** Indian nationals (including dependents) are in the employment-based queue, representing ~63% of the total EB backlog
- The Cato Institute estimates that at current processing rates, a new EB-2 India applicant faces a theoretical wait of **134 years**
- An estimated **424,000 applicants will die** of old age before receiving their green cards; over 90% of them Indian nationals

### China (Mainland Born): Significant but Shorter

- **EB-2 China FAD:** September 1, 2021 -- a **~5 year** backlog
- **EB-3 China FAD:** June 15, 2021 -- a **~5 year** backlog
- **EB-1 China FAD:** April 1, 2023 -- a **~3 year** backlog
- Tens of thousands of pending cases; typical waits of **5-8 years** for EB-2/EB-3
- EB-1 retrogression for China has been persistent since ~2018, unlike most other countries

### Rest of World: Mostly Current

- **EB-1:** Current (no wait)
- **EB-2:** Current as of April 2026
- **EB-3:** Current as of April 2026
- Applicants from countries other than India, China, Mexico, and the Philippines generally face minimal backlogs, often processing within **1-2 years** of filing

## 5. The 7% Per-Country Cap

Under INA Section 202, no single country of birth can receive more than **7% of the total employment-based immigrant visas** issued in a fiscal year. With ~140,000 EB visas annually, that is roughly **9,800 visas per country** (including dependents).

**Why this creates massive backlogs:**
- India produces far more EB applicants than 9,800 per year (driven by the tech industry's heavy H-1B usage)
- China similarly exceeds its allocation
- A small country with 50 applicants gets the same per-country cap as India with hundreds of thousands
- The result: identical qualifications, identical jobs, but wildly different wait times based solely on birthplace

**Example:** An Indian-born software engineer at Google with a 2024 EB-2 priority date faces a 100+ year theoretical wait. A Canadian-born colleague in the same role, same company, same filing year, gets a green card in under 2 years.

## 6. Visa Spillover Rules

When visa numbers go unused in higher preference categories, they "spill over" to lower ones:

1. **Family-to-Employment spillover:** Unused family-based visas from one fiscal year are added to the employment-based pool the following year. In FY2021, this generated ~60,000 extra EB visas (due to COVID-era consulate closures reducing FB usage).
2. **Downward cascade within EB:** Unused EB-1 visas fall to EB-2, then unused EB-2 visas fall to EB-3.
3. **Within-category spillover:** Once Rest of World demand is satisfied, unused per-country allocations become available to oversubscribed countries (primarily India).

**FY2027 outlook:** The [immigrant visa ban affecting 70+ countries](https://cilawgroup.com/news/2026/02/26/a-return-to-2021-why-the-new-immigrant-visa-ban-signals-historic-forward-movement-for-eb-backlogs/) is expected to depress FB visa usage in FY2026, potentially generating 60,000+ spillover visas for EB categories in FY2027. This could produce significant forward movement for India EB-2/EB-3.

## 7. Retrogression: When Dates Move Backward

**What it is:** Retrogression occurs when the State Department moves a priority date cutoff *backward* (earlier in time) from one month to the next. A date that was current last month may no longer be current.

**When it happens:**
- Most commonly in Q4 of the fiscal year (July-September) as annual visa limits are approached
- When USCIS receives a surge of I-485 filings in response to forward movement
- At the start of a new fiscal year (October), dates typically reset forward again

**Impact on pending cases:**
- If you already filed I-485 and your date retrogresses, your case is held in abeyance (not rejected)
- You retain your EAD work authorization and advance parole
- Your case resumes processing when your date becomes current again
- You do NOT lose your place in line

**Recent example:** After the April 2026 bulletin showed a 303-day leap for EB-2 India, immigration attorneys are warning of potential retrogression risk in Q3/Q4 FY2026 as a surge of new filings hits the system.

## 8. Country of Birth vs. Citizenship

Your green card queue is determined by **where you were born**, not:
- Your current citizenship
- Where you live now
- Where you last resided
- Your employer's location

**Key implications:**
- A person born in India who later became a Canadian citizen is still charged to India
- A person born in the U.S. (to Indian parents) who grew up in India is charged to the U.S. (no backlog)
- A person born in Hong Kong may be charged separately from mainland China (country of chargeability is determined by birth region for some cases)

This is codified in INA Section 202(b) and cannot be changed by individual petition -- it requires legislative action.

## 9. Strategies for Long Backlogs

### Category Upgrade

| Strategy | How It Helps | Requirements |
|----------|-------------|--------------|
| **EB-2 to EB-1A** | EB-1 India is Dec 2023 vs. EB-2 India at Jul 2014 -- saves ~9 years | Demonstrate extraordinary ability via published work, awards, high salary, media coverage, etc. |
| **EB-2 to EB-1B** | Same benefit as EB-1A | Outstanding professor/researcher with international recognition |
| **EB-2 to EB-1C** | Same benefit | Multinational manager/executive with 1+ year abroad in last 3 years |
| **EB-3 to EB-2** | Marginal benefit for India (dates are close) but significant for China | Master's degree or bachelor's + 5 years progressive experience |

### National Interest Waiver (EB-2 NIW)

- Self-petitioned (no employer sponsorship needed)
- No labor certification (PERM) required, saving 1-2 years of processing
- Same EB-2 backlog applies, but you can file earlier and independently
- Useful for career flexibility -- not tied to a specific employer

### Cross-Chargeability

- If your spouse was born in a non-backlogged country, you can use their country of birth
- Example: Indian-born applicant married to someone born in France can use France's allocation (Current)
- Only works with spouse or parents (not children)
- Must be claimed at the I-485 stage

### Concurrent Filing and Port-of-Entry Strategies

- File I-140 + I-485 concurrently when Dates for Filing are current
- Once I-485 is pending, you get EAD/AP benefits regardless of later retrogression
- Priority date portability: if you change jobs, your old priority date transfers to the new petition

### EB-5 Investor Visa

- Minimum $800,000 investment in a Targeted Employment Area
- Separate quota from EB-1/2/3; shorter backlog for India (though growing)
- Not subject to the same per-country dynamics for most applicants

## 11. Legislative Proposals

### EAGLE Act (Equal Access to Green Cards for Legal Employment)

- **What it does:** Phases out the 7% per-country cap on employment-based visas over a 9-year transition period; raises family-based cap to 15%
- **Status:** Reintroduced in the 119th Congress (2025-2026) as [H.R.1299](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/1299). Bipartisan sponsors include Sens. Cramer (R-ND) and Hickenlooper (D-CO).
- **History:** Passed the House in the 116th Congress but stalled in the Senate. Has been reintroduced in every Congress since.
- **Impact if passed:** Would dramatically reduce India wait times but temporarily increase waits for currently non-backlogged countries during the transition period.
- **Likelihood:** Uncertain. Immigration reform faces competing priorities, and opposition exists from groups concerned about the transition-period impact on smaller countries.

### Other Proposals

- **IVES Act:** Similar to EAGLE Act with additional provisions for visa recapture (recovering historically unused visas)
- **Visa recapture bills:** Would recover an estimated 200,000+ visas that went unused in prior fiscal years
- **Staple Act:** Would grant green cards to foreign STEM graduates of U.S. universities, bypassing the EB queue
- **DHS regulatory reforms:** In 2026, DHS announced plans for [employment-based green card process reforms](https://manifestlaw.com/blog/immigration/news/dhs-plans-major-employment-based-green-card-reforms-for-2026/) that could improve efficiency within the existing statutory framework

## 12. Key Takeaways

1. **The backlog is a birthplace lottery.** Two people with identical qualifications at the same company face wait times differing by a decade, based solely on country of birth.
2. **India EB-2/EB-3 is effectively broken.** A 134-year theoretical wait means the system cannot clear the existing backlog within any applicant's lifetime.
3. **China is strained but manageable.** A 5-8 year wait is painful but finite.
4. **Rest of World is essentially clear.** Most categories are Current.
5. **EB-1 is the pressure valve.** Even for India, EB-1 has a ~2.5 year backlog -- qualifying for EB-1 can save a decade of waiting.
6. **Legislative reform has stalled repeatedly.** The EAGLE Act has bipartisan support but has not passed in over 6 years of attempts.
7. **Spillover dynamics matter.** Policy changes that reduce family-based visa usage (travel bans, consulate closures) can create significant forward movement in EB categories the following year.
8. **Cross-chargeability is underused.** Applicants with spouses born in non-backlogged countries should explore this option immediately.
