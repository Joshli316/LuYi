# PERM Labor Certification: Complete Guide

> Research compiled April 2026. Data reflects DOL processing updates through March 2026.

---

## 1. What Is PERM and Why Is It Required?

PERM (Program Electronic Review Management) is the Department of Labor (DOL) system for processing permanent labor certification applications. It is the **mandatory first step** for most employer-sponsored green cards under the EB-2 (Advanced Degree / Exceptional Ability) and EB-3 (Professional / Skilled Worker / Other Worker) categories.

**Purpose:** The DOL must certify that:

- No qualified, willing, and available U.S. workers exist for the position
- Hiring a foreign worker will not adversely affect wages or working conditions of similarly employed U.S. workers
- The job is permanent and full-time
- The offered wage meets or exceeds the prevailing wage

**Who needs PERM:**

| Category | PERM Required? |
|----------|---------------|
| EB-2 Advanced Degree (employer-sponsored) | Yes |
| EB-2 Exceptional Ability (employer-sponsored) | Yes |
| EB-2 NIW (National Interest Waiver) | **No** |
| EB-3 Professional (bachelor's degree) | Yes |
| EB-3 Skilled Worker (2+ years experience) | Yes |
| EB-3 Other Worker (<2 years experience) | Yes |
| EB-1A, EB-1B, EB-1C | **No** |

The DOL does not distinguish between EB-2 and EB-3 when reviewing PERM applications. The EB-2 vs. EB-3 classification only matters at the later I-140 petition stage with USCIS.

## 2. Step-by-Step Process

The PERM process has three major phases before the DOL filing itself:

### Phase 1: Prevailing Wage Determination (PWD)

1. Employer identifies the job title, duties, requirements, and work location
2. Employer files Form ETA-9141 with DOL's National Prevailing Wage Center (NPWC)
3. DOL assigns an SOC (Standard Occupational Classification) code and determines the prevailing wage based on occupation, location, and skill level
4. Employer receives the PWD (valid for 1 year from determination date; in some cases extended to 2 years)

### Phase 2: Recruitment (Labor Market Test)

5. Employer posts a **job order** with the State Workforce Agency (SWA) for a minimum of 30 days
6. Employer places **two Sunday newspaper advertisements** in a paper of general circulation at the place of employment
7. Employer posts an **internal Notice of Filing** at the worksite for 10 consecutive business days
8. For professional positions (requiring a bachelor's degree or higher), employer must complete **3 additional recruitment steps** from a list of 10 options (see Section 5)
9. Employer reviews all applicants using lawful, job-related reasons for rejection
10. Employer prepares a **recruitment report** documenting all efforts and outcomes

The recruitment window must last at least **30 days** but not exceed **180 days** before filing.

### Phase 3: PERM Filing and Adjudication

11. Employer files **Form ETA-9089** electronically through the FLAG system
12. DOL reviews the application (Analyst Review track or random Audit track)
13. If approved, employer receives certified labor certification
14. Employer files **I-140 Immigrant Petition** with USCIS within 180 days of PERM certification

## 3. Current Processing Times (as of March 2026)

| Stage | Current Timeline |
|-------|-----------------|
| Prevailing Wage Determination | ~3 months (improved from 5-7 months in 2025) |
| Recruitment phase | 2-4 months |
| PERM filing to decision (no audit) | ~503 days / ~16.5 months |
| PERM filing to decision (with audit) | 22-28 months |
| **Total end-to-end (no audit)** | **22-26 months** |
| **Total end-to-end (with audit)** | **28-38 months** |

**DOL adjudication status (March 2026):**

- Analyst Review: processing cases filed **October 2024 or earlier**
- Audit Review: processing audited cases filed **June 2025 or earlier**
- Reconsideration requests: filed **September 2025 or earlier**

**Application volume trends:**

- FY 2024: 151,773 total applications (down 4.5% from FY 2023)
- FY 2025 Q1: 34,186 applications (down 17.6% from FY 2024 Q1)
- Approval rate: ~95%+ (denial rate 4.02% in FY 2025 Q1)

## 4. Prevailing Wage Determination and Four Wage Levels

The DOL uses data from the Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) survey to calculate prevailing wages in a four-tier system based on job complexity and experience required.

### Current Wage Levels (in effect)

| Level | Description | OES Percentile |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Level I | Entry-level, closely supervised | 35th percentile |
| Level II | Qualified, limited judgment | 53rd percentile |
| Level III | Experienced, independent judgment | 72nd percentile |
| Level IV | Fully competent, expert | 90th percentile |

### Proposed Rule Change (March 2026 NPRM)

On March 26, 2026, DOL proposed significant increases:

| Level | Current | Proposed | Change |
|-------|---------|----------|--------|
| Level I | 35th percentile | 34th percentile | ~same |
| Level II | 53rd percentile | 52nd percentile | ~same |
| Level III | 72nd percentile | 70th percentile | slight decrease |
| Level IV | 90th percentile | 88th percentile | slight decrease |

> **Note:** The March 2026 NPRM references increases from the *pre-2021 baseline* (17th/34th/50th/67th percentiles). Compared to the current rule already in effect (35th/53rd/72nd/90th), the proposed levels are similar or slightly lower. The comment period closes May 26, 2026. If finalized, the estimated average wage increase would be ~$14,000/year per worker relative to the older baseline.

**Key factors that determine wage level:**

- Job complexity and supervision required
- Education and experience requirements
- Special skills or certifications needed
- Geographic location (metro area)

## 5. Recruitment Requirements

### Mandatory for All Positions

| Step | Requirement |
|------|------------|
| Job Order | 30-day posting with State Workforce Agency (SWA) |
| Newspaper Ads | Two Sunday ads in newspaper of general circulation |
| Notice of Filing | Posted at worksite for 10 consecutive business days |

### Additional Steps for Professional Positions (3 of 10 required)

1. Job fairs
2. Employer's website posting
3. Job search website (e.g., Indeed, LinkedIn)
4. On-campus recruiting
5. Trade or professional organization postings
6. Private employment firms
7. Employee referral program with incentives
8. Campus placement offices
9. Local and ethnic newspapers
10. Radio and television advertisements

### Documentation Required

- Recruitment report with all applicant names, resumes received, and lawful job-related reasons for any rejections
- Copies of all advertisements with dates
- Evidence of SWA job order
- Internal posting evidence
- All correspondence with applicants

## 6. Audit Rates and Triggers

**Audit rate:** Approximately **25-30%** of PERM applications are audited. DOL randomly selects ~30% per fiscal year, plus targeted audits based on red flags.

**Common audit triggers:**

- **Wage discrepancies** — offered wage doesn't match PWD or market norms
- **SOC code mismatch** — job duties inconsistent with assigned occupation code
- **Unusual job requirements** — requirements that appear tailored to the foreign worker
- **Foreign language requirement** — flagged unless clear business necessity documented
- **Familial relationship** — employer and worker are related (nearly guaranteed audit)
- **Small company size** — fewer resources to demonstrate genuine recruitment
- **Inconsistencies** — mismatches between PWD, PERM application, and recruitment materials
- **Worker qualifications gap** — beneficiary's background doesn't clearly match stated requirements

**Audit response:**

- 30 days to respond once audit notice received
- Must provide complete recruitment documentation, signed recruitment report, copies of ads, and any additional evidence requested
- Failure to respond or incomplete response = denial
- Audits add **6-12 months** to processing timeline

## 7. Costs (Employer-Paid)

By law, the employer must pay all PERM-related costs and **cannot seek reimbursement from the employee** (per 2007 DOL regulation). If employer and employee share the same attorney, the employer pays.

| Cost Component | Typical Range |
|---------------|--------------|
| Attorney fees (PERM stage) | $2,000 - $6,000 |
| Recruitment advertising | $1,000 - $6,000 |
| DOL filing fee (Form ETA-9089) | **$0** (no fee) |
| Audit response (if triggered) | $1,500 - $2,500 additional |
| **Total PERM stage** | **$5,000 - $15,000** |

**Subsequent costs (not part of PERM, but part of green card process):**

| Stage | Cost |
|-------|------|
| I-140 filing fee (USCIS) | $715 |
| I-140 premium processing (optional) | $2,805 |
| I-485 filing fee (per person) | $1,440 |
| Attorney fees (I-140 + I-485) | $3,000 - $8,000 |

> The employee may pay their own attorney fees only if they have separate legal counsel representing them individually (not shared with the employer).

## 8. Common Denial Reasons

Despite a 95%+ approval rate, denials do occur. Top reasons include:

1. **Recruitment deficiencies** — missing ads, wrong dates, incomplete job order, failure to use required number of additional recruitment methods
2. **Notice of Filing errors** — not posted for full 10 business days, wrong location, incomplete information
3. **Job requirements appear tailored** — requirements exceed what the position truly needs, suggesting they were designed for a specific individual
4. **Prevailing wage issues** — offered wage below PWD, misclassified job title or location
5. **Inadequate audit response** — incomplete documentation, late submission, missing recruitment report
6. **Beneficiary qualifications gap** — education, training, or experience does not clearly meet stated minimum requirements
7. **Application inconsistencies** — conflicting information between ETA-9089, PWD, and recruitment materials
8. **Expired recruitment** — recruitment conducted more than 180 days before filing
9. **Expired PWD** — prevailing wage determination expired before PERM filing
10. **Failure to respond to audit** — automatic denial

**After denial:** Employers may file a request for reconsideration with the Board of Alien Labor Certification Appeals (BALCA) or refile a new PERM application.

## 9. PERM Portability: Changing Employers

**PERM itself is NOT portable.** The labor certification is tied to a specific employer and specific job. However, there are key portability provisions at later stages:

### Before I-140 Approval

- Changing employers = **start PERM over from scratch** with new employer
- No priority date retention unless prior I-140 was approved

### After I-140 Approval (AC21 Portability)

Under the American Competitiveness in the Twenty-First Century Act (AC21):

| Condition | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| I-140 approved + I-485 pending 180+ days | Can port to new employer |
| New job requirement | Must be same or similar occupational classification |
| New PERM/I-140 needed? | **No** — file I-485 Supplement J only |
| Priority date | **Retained** even if original employer withdraws I-140 |
| Original employer withdraws I-140 before 180 days | Priority date and case are **lost** |

### Priority Date Retention

- An approved I-140 preserves the priority date even if the petition is later withdrawn or revoked (after 180 days of I-485 pending)
- This priority date can be used with a new employer's petition

## 10. Recent Rule Changes and DOL Backlogs

### PERM Modernization (FLAG System)

DOL is transitioning PERM to the **Foreign Labor Application Gateway (FLAG)**, introducing a revised Form ETA-9089 designed to streamline processing and improve transparency.

### Proposed Prevailing Wage Rule (March 2026)

- Published March 26, 2026 as a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking
- Would adjust wage level percentiles for H-1B, H-1B1, E-3, and PERM
- Comment period closes May 26, 2026
- If finalized, entry-level salary requirements could increase 30%+ from pre-2021 levels
- Would not apply retroactively

### Backlog Reality

- DOL Analyst Review averages **503 days** (16.5 months) as of March 2026
- Audited cases face even longer waits
- Total end-to-end PERM timelines now average **22-26 months** without audit
- Application volume is declining (down 17.6% in FY 2025 Q1), but backlogs persist

## 11. Alternatives That Skip PERM

For workers who may qualify, several green card categories bypass the PERM labor certification entirely:

| Category | PERM Required? | Employer Required? | Key Requirement |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **EB-1A** Extraordinary Ability | No | No (self-petition) | Sustained national/international acclaim; meet 3 of 10 criteria |
| **EB-1B** Outstanding Researcher/Professor | No | Yes | 3+ years research/teaching; international recognition |
| **EB-1C** Multinational Manager/Executive | No | Yes | 1+ year with overseas affiliate in managerial/executive role |
| **EB-2 NIW** National Interest Waiver | No | No (self-petition) | Substantial merit + national importance; well-positioned to advance endeavor |
| **Schedule A** Group I | No | Yes | Registered nurses and physical therapists with state license |
| **Schedule A** Group II | No | Yes | Persons of exceptional ability in sciences or arts |

### Time Savings

- EB-1A/EB-2 NIW: Skip PERM entirely (saves 22-26 months), can self-petition
- EB-1B: Skip PERM but still need employer sponsorship
- Schedule A: Skip labor market test; file I-140 directly with USCIS
- At the start of 2026, EB-1 wait times are nearly **two years faster** than EB-2

### Dual-Filing Strategy

Many immigration attorneys recommend filing EB-2 NIW or EB-1A concurrently with PERM as a hedge — if the self-petition is approved first, the PERM case becomes unnecessary. If the self-petition is denied, the PERM case provides a backup path.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **PERM is slow.** Budget 22-26 months minimum (28-38 with audit) from start to PERM approval alone — before I-140 and I-485.
2. **Employer bears all PERM costs** ($5,000-$15,000) and cannot pass them to the employee.
3. **Audit risk is real** (~30% of cases) and adds 6-12 months. Meticulous documentation is the best defense.
4. **PERM is not portable.** Changing employers before I-140 approval means starting over.
5. **Explore alternatives first.** EB-1A, EB-2 NIW, and Schedule A all skip PERM entirely and can save years.
6. **Start early.** Given current backlogs, employers should begin PERM processes as early as possible.
