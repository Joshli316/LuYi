# EB-2 NIW (National Interest Waiver) Deep Dive

*Research date: April 7, 2026*

---

## 1. Matter of Dhanasar: The Three-Prong Test

On December 27, 2016, the AAO issued *Matter of Dhanasar*, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016), replacing the prior *NYSDOT* framework with a more flexible three-prong test for NIW eligibility.

| Prong | Question | Key Concept |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **1** | Does the proposed endeavor have substantial merit and national importance? | The *what* -- your work matters and benefits the U.S. broadly |
| **2** | Is the petitioner well positioned to advance the endeavor? | The *who* -- you specifically can deliver results |
| **3** | On balance, would the U.S. benefit from waiving the job offer and labor certification requirements? | The *why waive* -- hiring you this way is better than the normal process |

**Critical distinction**: Your "endeavor" must be more specific than your general occupation. "I am a software engineer" is insufficient. "I am developing AI-driven diagnostic tools for early cancer detection in underserved U.S. communities" is the level of specificity USCIS expects.

---

## 2. Evidence Requirements for Each Prong

### Prong 1: Substantial Merit and National Importance

**Substantial merit** can be demonstrated across business, science, technology, health, education, or culture -- without requiring immediate economic impact.

**National importance** is the harder bar. Evidence must show impact beyond a single employer, institution, or locality.

| Strong Evidence | Weak Evidence |
|----------------|---------------|
| Patents adopted by multiple companies | Job description at a single company |
| Published research cited by other labs | Teaching a single classroom |
| Government grants or contracts | Work benefiting only one employer |
| Innovations with broader industry implications | Vague claims of "advancing the field" |
| Policy influence or standards contributions | Localized impact with no scaling plan |

### Prong 2: Well Positioned to Advance the Endeavor

USCIS considers 15+ evidence types, including:

- Advanced degrees (Ph.D. strongly favored)
- Patents (filed or granted)
- Peer-reviewed publications and citation counts
- Research funding and government grants
- Accelerator acceptance or venture funding
- Record of success in related efforts
- Concrete business plan or research roadmap
- Letters of recommendation (must be corroborated by independent evidence)
- Progress already made toward the endeavor

**2025-2026 warning**: Business plans and expert letters alone carry little weight. Officers now demand independent, objective corroboration for every major claim.

### Prong 3: Balancing Test

This prong asks whether it would benefit the U.S. to waive the labor certification process. Favorable factors:

- Urgency of the work (e.g., national security, public health)
- Impracticality of a labor certification for the specific endeavor
- STEM Ph.D.s in critical and emerging technologies receive especially favorable consideration
- Letters from U.S. government agencies explaining urgency can be "almost decisive"
- Self-employment or entrepreneurial endeavors where a traditional job offer is impractical

## 5. Common RFE Reasons

Ranked by frequency in 2025-2026:

1. **National importance not established** -- Most common. Work described as too local, too narrow, or benefiting only one employer.
2. **Endeavor too vague or generic** -- Recycled language, broad field descriptions instead of specific proposed work.
3. **Insufficient independent corroboration** -- Claims of adoption, commercialization, or impact lack third-party documentation.
4. **Weak Prong 2 evidence** -- Expert letters unsupported by objective evidence; no concrete progress shown.
5. **Qualification mismatch** -- Advanced degree or exceptional ability not clearly linked to the proposed endeavor.
6. **Cross-reference inconsistencies** (new in 2025-2026) -- Officers cross-check I-140 materials against prior government submissions; mismatched dates, titles, or employer names trigger credibility RFEs.
7. **Residential address / biometric requests** (new since April 2025) -- Some petitioners receiving unexpected requests for address histories and fingerprints.

## 6. Processing Times

| Stage | Regular Processing | Premium Processing |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **I-140 petition** | 18-20 months (as of early 2026) | 45 calendar days (median ~21 days in practice) |
| **I-485 adjustment** | 8-14 months after I-140 approval | Not available for I-485 |
| **Total to green card** | 2-3+ years | 1.5-2+ years (premium only speeds I-140) |

**Premium processing fee**: $2,965 (effective March 1, 2026; up from $2,805).

**Important**: Premium processing only speeds the I-140 decision. It does not affect visa availability, priority date position, or I-485 processing time.

**April 2026 update**: EB-2 ROW (Rest of World) dates are currently "Current" on both the Final Action and Dates for Filing charts, enabling concurrent I-140 + I-485 filing. This is historically volatile and may retrogress without warning.

## 8. NIW While on OPT: Self-Petition Rules

**Yes, you can self-petition for EB-2 NIW while on F-1 OPT or STEM OPT.** Key rules:

- **No employer sponsorship needed.** NIW is a self-petition -- you file Form I-140 on your own behalf.
- **I-140 alone does not invalidate F-1 status**, provided you select consular processing (not adjustment of status) on the form.
- **Dual intent risk**: F-1 is a non-dual-intent visa. Filing I-140 demonstrates immigrant intent. This can create problems if you later need to renew your F-1 visa at a consulate, apply for a new OPT/STEM OPT extension, or switch to another non-dual-intent status (e.g., TN).
- **Safest approach**: File I-140 only (no concurrent I-485) while on OPT. Select consular processing. Do not travel internationally if possible until you have a backup status strategy.
- **If visa becomes current**: You can file I-485 later. As of April 2026, EB-2 ROW is current, meaning concurrent filing is possible -- but this changes F-1 status considerations significantly.
- **Denial does not affect OPT**: A denied NIW petition does not automatically affect your STEM OPT extension or F-1 status.

## 9. NIW vs EB-1A: When to Choose Which

| Factor | EB-2 NIW | EB-1A |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Standard** | National interest (Dhanasar 3-prong) | Extraordinary ability (3 of 10 criteria) |
| **Evidence focus** | Proposed endeavor + future impact | Past achievements + sustained acclaim |
| **Self-petition** | Yes | Yes |
| **Employer needed** | No | No |
| **Degree required** | Advanced degree or exceptional ability | No degree required (evidence-based) |
| **Visa category** | EB-2 (second preference) | EB-1 (first preference, faster dates) |
| **Approval rate (Q1 FY2025)** | ~62.8% | ~74.9% |
| **Processing time** | 18-20 months (regular) | 6-12 months (regular) |
| **Best for** | Mid-career STEM professionals, Ph.D. students, entrepreneurs | Senior researchers, award winners, widely cited scholars |

**Choose NIW if**: You have an advanced degree and impactful work but lack the top-tier recognition EB-1A demands. Good for Ph.D. students/postdocs, mid-career engineers, and entrepreneurs with traction.

**Choose EB-1A if**: You have major awards, high citation counts (500+), media coverage, judging roles, and leadership positions. EB-1A offers faster visa availability as a first-preference category.

**File both if possible**: Many attorneys recommend filing NIW first to lock in a priority date, then filing EB-1A as your profile strengthens. If EB-1A is approved, you can port your earlier NIW priority date to the EB-1 category.

## 10. Recent 2025-2026 Policy Changes

### January 15, 2025: Major USCIS Policy Manual Update
- Most detailed NIW guidance in nearly a decade, effective immediately
- Does not change the Dhanasar test but specifies how officers apply it
- Demands specificity: "endeavor" must be narrower than general occupation
- Exceptional ability must relate directly to the proposed endeavor
- Business plans and letters require independent corroboration

### Stricter Adjudication Climate (2025-2026)
- Approval rates dropped from 71% (FY2024) to 55.2% (FY2025) to 35.7% (Q4 FY2025)
- RFE rates increased significantly
- Officers cross-reference I-140 materials against prior submissions
- Some petitioners receiving biometric and address history requests (since April 2025)

### Field-Specific Shifts Under Trump Administration
- **AI/ML**: Strongly favored. Federal AI prioritization directly informs "national importance" analysis.
- **Renewable energy/climate**: More challenging framing. Petitioners should emphasize energy independence, economic competitiveness, or technological innovation -- not climate activism.
- **DEI-related work**: Executive orders rescinding DEI programs create headwinds. Reframe around workforce development, educational outcomes, or economic impact.
- **National security / defense**: Strongly favored under current administration priorities.

### April 2026 Visa Bulletin
- EB-2 ROW is Current on both Final Action and Dates for Filing charts
- Concurrent I-140 + I-485 filing now possible for ROW applicants
- Dates are historically volatile; no guarantee of remaining current

## Key Takeaways for International Students

1. **Start building your evidence portfolio now.** Publications, patents, conference presentations, and collaborations all count.
2. **STEM Ph.D.s remain the strongest candidates**, but the bar is rising for everyone.
3. **Approval rates have dropped dramatically** since 2022. A strong petition is no longer optional -- it is essential.
4. **You can self-petition on OPT**, but manage dual-intent risk carefully.
5. **Consider filing both NIW and EB-1A** to maximize your chances and lock in an early priority date.
6. **Frame your endeavor around current policy priorities**: AI, national security, semiconductor manufacturing, healthcare innovation, energy independence.
7. **Budget $10,000-$20,000** total (including attorney fees and premium processing).
8. **April 2026 is a window**: EB-2 ROW dates are current, enabling concurrent filing. This window may close.
