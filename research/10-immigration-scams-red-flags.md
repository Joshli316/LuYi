# Immigration Scams & Red Flags: Protecting International Students

> Research report for LuYi -- compiled April 2026. Covers common scam types, verification methods, red flags, reporting channels, and real cases.

---

## 1. Common Scam Types Targeting International Students

### Government Impersonation Scams

The most widespread scam. Fraudsters call, email, or text students claiming to be from USCIS, DHS, IRS, SSA, or local police. They use spoofed phone numbers, official-sounding names, and fake documents with seals. The script is consistent: "You are out of status" or "You owe taxes" -- then a demand for immediate payment via wire transfer, gift cards, or cryptocurrency.

In 2025, the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) issued a specific alert about impersonation schemes targeting Middle Eastern students, and USC's Office of International Services issued a campus-wide fraud warning. Around one-third of all scams reported by international students involved fraudulent government messages tied to visas or taxes.

**Key fact:** USCIS never calls to demand immediate payment. They communicate primarily by mail through official notices.

### Notario Fraud / Unauthorized Practice of Immigration Law

In many Latin American countries, a "notario publico" is a licensed legal professional. In the U.S., a notary public can only witness signatures -- they cannot give legal advice. Scammers exploit this confusion by advertising as "notarios" and offering immigration services they are not qualified to provide.

| Notario Fraud Pattern | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Promise "guaranteed" green cards | File incorrect or fraudulent applications |
| Charge $3,000-$10,000+ | Provide no real legal representation |
| Submit false information | Client's immigration status is jeopardized |
| Disappear after payment | Victim left with damaged case, no recourse |

The scale is significant: immigrants have filed hundreds of complaints across South Carolina, Texas, Washington, Rhode Island, Missouri, and Illinois. National losses are estimated at $1.2 million or more annually. In June 2025, the New York City Council passed the nation's most comprehensive anti-notario legislation, doubling civil penalties to $7,500-$10,000 per violation.

### Fake Law Firms and Fraudulent Attorneys

Some operations go beyond notarios -- they impersonate actual law firms or create fictitious ones. Red flags include:

- No verifiable state bar license number
- Operating out of informal locations (strip malls, shared offices)
- Advertising only in ethnic-language media with no English web presence
- Refusing to provide a signed retainer agreement

AARP has documented cases where criminals posed as lawyers, collected thousands in fees, filed nothing, and vanished.

### Pay-to-Stay Schools and Visa Mills

Fraudulent schools maintain SEVP certification while enrolling students who never attend classes, enabling them to maintain F-1 visa status. Notable cases:

- **Silicon Valley University (SVU):** In 2015, hundreds of Indian students were denied entry by CBP. Students reportedly worked at gas stations and restaurants instead of attending classes. Despite this, SVU retained its SEVP certification for years.
- **Northwestern Polytechnic University (NPU):** A 2014 whistleblower reported grade falsification to keep foreign students in visa status. Enrollment surged 350% to 9,026 students. NPU kept its SEVP certification despite multiple fraud reports.
- **University of Farmington** (see Section 6 below for full case study).

Senator Chuck Grassley's Judiciary Committee investigation found that SEVP waited until the very end of investigations or criminal prosecutions before acting against schools, exposing students and national security to prolonged risk.

### Fake Job Offers and OPT/CPT Fraud

International students on F-1 visas are especially vulnerable during OPT and STEM OPT periods when they must find employment to maintain status. Scam patterns include:

- **Profile marketing firms** that charge $1,500-$2,000 to "market" a student's resume, collect money from 50+ students, and vanish
- **Fake CPT employers** that issue training agreements for jobs that don't exist
- **Shell companies** that "hire" students on paper but provide no actual work -- just enough documentation to maintain SEVIS records
- **H-1B bait-and-switch** -- companies promise sponsorship, extract months of below-market labor, then abandon the petition

**Consequences for students:** SEVIS termination, loss of status, potential permanent inadmissibility to the U.S.

### DV Lottery Fee Scams

The Diversity Visa (DV) Lottery is free to enter (a $1 registration fee was introduced in September 2025). Scammers exploit this by:

- Creating fake lottery websites that charge $50-$500 to "submit" entries
- Sending fake "winner" notification emails demanding processing fees
- In Uzbekistan and other countries, brokers submit entries without applicants' knowledge, then demand payment to release confirmation numbers

In DV-2025, the State Department discovered **2.5 million fraudulent entries**. The only legitimate entry site is `dvprogram.state.gov`. The State Department never sends emails, letters, or phone calls to notify winners.

### Document Fraud

Purchasing or using fake green cards, work permits, Social Security cards, or diplomas carries severe consequences under 18 USC 1546:

- **5-25 years** federal imprisonment depending on circumstances
- A green card is valued at **$1.3 million** for sentencing purposes (as of Nov 2024)
- **Permanent inadmissibility** under INA Section 212(a)(6)(C) -- no time limit on discovery
- Even decades-old fraud can trigger removal proceedings

## 2. How to Verify an Immigration Attorney

Before hiring anyone for immigration legal services, verify through multiple channels:

| Verification Step | How to Do It |
|---|---|
| **State bar license** | Search your state's bar association website (e.g., calbar.ca.gov, nysba.org). Confirm the attorney is "Active" with no disciplinary actions. |
| **AILA membership** | Contact the American Immigration Lawyers Association (aila.org) to confirm membership. AILA membership indicates specialization in immigration law. Note: AILA is voluntary and does not discipline lawyers. |
| **EOIR registration** | Check the Executive Office for Immigration Review's list of practitioners authorized to appear before immigration courts. |
| **Disciplinary history** | Search state bar discipline records AND check EOIR's disciplinary actions page. Discipline by one authority does not automatically trigger action by another. |
| **DOJ accredited representative** | Non-attorneys can represent immigrants only if they are DOJ-accredited representatives working for DOJ-recognized organizations. Verify at justice.gov/eoir. |

**Where to file complaints about attorneys:**
- State bar where the attorney is licensed
- EOIR Fraud and Abuse Prevention Program: 877-388-3840 or eoir.fraud.program@usdoj.gov
- DHS disciplinary counsel (if the matter was before DHS/USCIS)

## 3. Red Flags with Immigration Lawyers

Even licensed attorneys can engage in fraud. Watch for these warning signs:

### Guaranteed Outcomes
- "I guarantee your green card will be approved"
- "I have special connections at USCIS"
- No attorney can guarantee immigration outcomes. USCIS decisions are made by government officers following federal law.

### Fee Problems
- Demanding full payment upfront with no written retainer agreement
- Cash-only payments with no receipts
- Charging for USCIS forms (all forms are free at uscis.gov)
- "Rush" or "expedite" fees for services that don't exist
- Fees dramatically below market rate (may indicate volume-mill that files cookie-cutter applications)

### Pressure Tactics
- "You must sign today or you'll be deported"
- "This special program closes tomorrow"
- Refusing to give you time to review documents or seek a second opinion
- Creating artificial urgency around deadlines that don't exist

### Blank Forms and Document Issues
- Asking you to sign blank forms or applications
- Asking you to lie on any form (material misrepresentation = permanent immigration bar)
- Not providing copies of everything filed on your behalf
- Filing documents without your review or signature

### Communication Failures
- Won't provide their bar number when asked
- No written fee agreement
- Unreachable after payment
- Won't explain your case strategy in terms you understand

## 4. H-1B Employer Red Flags

International students transitioning from OPT to H-1B status are particularly vulnerable to exploitative employers.

| Red Flag | Why It's a Problem |
|---|---|
| **Benching** (no pay between projects) | Illegal. H-1B employers must pay the prevailing wage from day one, regardless of available work. DOL has launched 200+ investigations in 2025. |
| **Employee pays filing fees** | Illegal. Under DOL regulations, the employer must pay all H-1B petition costs, including filing fees and attorney fees. The $100,000 H-1B fee introduced in September 2025 is also the employer's responsibility. |
| **Holding your passport or documents** | A form of coercion. You have the right to possess your own identity documents at all times. |
| **Repayment/penalty clauses** | Contracts requiring you to repay H-1B costs if you leave are legally questionable and used to trap workers. |
| **No actual job site** | If the employer cannot confirm your work location, duties, and supervisor, the petition may be fraudulent. FDNS site visits have more than doubled. |
| **Below-market wages** | H-1B requires payment of at least the prevailing wage. If offered significantly less, the employer may be committing wage fraud. |
| **Threats of deportation** | "If you quit, I'll revoke your visa" is a coercion tactic. While an employer can withdraw a petition, you have a grace period and legal options. |

**2025 Enforcement update:** The DOL launched "Project Firewall," a new initiative using secretary-certified investigations where there is "reasonable cause" to believe an H-1B employer is out of compliance. FDNS (Fraud Detection and National Security) site visits have more than doubled compared to previous years.

## 5. How to Report Immigration Fraud

| Agency | What to Report | How to Report |
|---|---|---|
| **USCIS** | Immigration benefit fraud, scams impersonating USCIS | Online Tip Form at uscis.gov/report-fraud (anonymous tips accepted) |
| **ICE / HSI** | Human smuggling, trafficking, employer exploitation | HSI Tip Line: **866-347-2423** |
| **DHS OIG** | Government employee misconduct, waste, fraud | oig.dhs.gov/hotline |
| **EOIR** | Fraud in immigration court proceedings | **877-388-3840** or eoir.fraud.program@usdoj.gov |
| **FTC** | General consumer fraud, scam businesses | ReportFraud.ftc.gov or **877-FTC-HELP** |
| **State Attorney General** | Notario fraud, unauthorized practice of law, consumer protection | Search "[your state] attorney general consumer complaint" |
| **State Bar** | Licensed attorney misconduct | Your state's bar association disciplinary office |
| **FBI IC3** | Internet-based scams, phishing | ic3.gov |

**Important:** You can report anonymously to most of these agencies. Your immigration status should not prevent you from reporting fraud. Many states have laws protecting victims of notario fraud regardless of immigration status.

## 6. Real Cases of Scams Affecting Students

### University of Farmington (Operation Paper Chase, 2015-2019)

The most notorious case. ICE created a fully fake university in Farmington Hills, Michigan as a sting operation:

- **The setup:** The "university" had a website, an office in a suburban business park, SEVP certification, and accreditation from two boards. It appeared on the DHS website as legitimate. Federal agents posing as university officials encouraged students to enroll and pay tuition.
- **The scale:** 600+ individuals identified. 130 students arrested (129 from India). Eight recruiters charged with visa fraud and harboring aliens for profit, earning $250,000+ in compensation.
- **Financial damage:** ICE collected $6 million in tuition without ever holding a single class.
- **Aftermath:** Students lost visas, some were detained, many were deported. In 2024, a federal appeals court ruled that students could sue the U.S. government for breach of contract, reinstating a class-action lawsuit.
- **Controversy:** Civil rights organizations argued the government itself created a trap with all the trappings of legitimacy, preying on students' desperation to maintain status.

**Lesson for students:** Even if a school appears on government websites and has proper accreditation, verify independently. Contact the school's international student office, visit the campus, confirm classes actually exist, and check reviews from current students.

### IC3 Alert: Middle Eastern Student Impersonation Scheme (2025)

In May 2025, the FBI IC3 issued a public service announcement about scammers specifically targeting Middle Eastern students in the U.S., using impersonation of government officials to extract payments.

### OPT Fraud Crackdown (2025)

Multiple cases of fraudulent companies charging F-1 students $1,500-$2,000 for fake job placements, fake work experience, and fraudulent STEM OPT employment verification letters. Some companies collected fees from 50+ students before disappearing. Students caught in these schemes faced SEVIS termination and potential bars from future U.S. visas.

### DV Lottery Fraud (Ongoing)

The State Department identified 2.5 million fraudulent DV-2025 entries, many submitted by criminal enterprises in Central Asia and Africa who later extorted applicants for fees to "release" their confirmation numbers.

## 7. Protecting Yourself: Quick Reference

### Before You Hire Anyone
- [ ] Verify their state bar license online
- [ ] Ask for their bar number and check it
- [ ] Get a written retainer agreement before paying anything
- [ ] Get receipts for all payments
- [ ] Never sign blank forms
- [ ] Never pay for forms available free at uscis.gov
- [ ] Get a second opinion if anything feels wrong

### If Something Goes Wrong
- [ ] Keep all documents, receipts, emails, and texts
- [ ] Report to the relevant agency (see table above)
- [ ] Consult a legitimate immigration attorney -- many offer free consultations
- [ ] Contact your university's international student office
- [ ] Do not assume reporting will hurt your immigration status

### Free and Low-Cost Legal Help
- Your university's international student services office
- Legal aid organizations in your area
- DOJ-recognized organizations with accredited representatives (list at justice.gov/eoir)
- AILA lawyer referral service (aila.org)
- CLINIC's legal services directory (cliniclegal.org)
