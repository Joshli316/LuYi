# Employer Sponsorship Guide: What International Students Must Ask Before Accepting a Job Offer

> Research compiled April 2026. Covers H-1B, green card, PERM, AC21 portability, and negotiation strategy.

---

## 1. Key Questions to Ask Employers About Sponsorship

Before accepting any offer, get clear answers to these questions — ideally in writing.

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|---------------|
| 1 | **Does the company sponsor H-1B visas?** | Some companies have blanket no-sponsorship policies. Know before you invest interview time. |
| 2 | **When will you file my H-1B petition?** | The annual lottery registration opens in March. Missing it means waiting another year. |
| 3 | **Will the company pay for premium processing?** | Premium processing guarantees a USCIS response within 15 business days. The fee is **$2,965** as of March 2026. |
| 4 | **Who pays the filing fees and attorney fees?** | By law, the employer must pay the base filing fee ($780), ACWIA fee ($750/$1,500), anti-fraud fee ($500), and asylum fee ($600). The employee **cannot** be required to pay these. |
| 5 | **Which immigration law firm does the company use?** | A dedicated, experienced firm signals a mature sponsorship program. Ask for the firm name so you can check their track record. |
| 6 | **Does the company sponsor for green cards? When does that process start?** | Some employers begin PERM within 1-2 years; others never do. This determines your long-term stability in the U.S. |
| 7 | **What is the company's H-1B approval rate?** | Check the [USCIS H-1B Employer Data Hub](https://www.uscis.gov/tools/reports-and-studies/h-1b-employer-data-hub) and [H1B Grader](https://h1bgrader.com/) for any employer's historical approval rate, denial rate, and wage levels. |
| 8 | **Will the company cover my spouse's H-4 filing?** | Dependent visa costs add up. Good employers cover these too. |

## 2. Green Flags: Signs of a Strong Sponsor

These indicators suggest a company has a reliable, employee-friendly immigration program.

- **Established sponsorship history.** The company has filed H-1Bs for multiple years with high approval rates (check USCIS Data Hub for verification).
- **Dedicated immigration legal team.** Either in-house immigration counsel or a long-standing relationship with a reputable immigration law firm.
- **Employer pays all fees.** Base filing fees, attorney fees, PERM recruitment costs, and premium processing are covered by the company.
- **Early PERM filing.** The company initiates the green card process (PERM labor certification) within 12-18 months of employment rather than waiting for the employee to ask.
- **Supports H-1B transfers in.** Willingness to file a transfer petition for candidates already on H-1B with another employer indicates process maturity.
- **Written sponsorship commitment.** The offer letter or employment contract explicitly mentions H-1B and green card sponsorship timelines.
- **Cap-exempt status.** Universities, nonprofit research organizations, and government research labs can sponsor H-1Bs year-round without the lottery — a massive advantage.
- **High wage levels (Level II-IV).** Companies filing at higher prevailing wage levels have better lottery odds under the wage-prioritized selection system and signal genuine full-time roles.

## 3. Red Flags: Warning Signs to Walk Away From

| Red Flag | What It Really Means |
|----------|---------------------|
| **"We'll look into sponsorship after a year"** | No commitment. You may exhaust your OPT with nothing filed. |
| **Employee pays filing fees** | Illegal under federal law. The DOL considers this an "illegal assumption of costs." Violations can result in fines, restitution, and debarment from the H-1B program. |
| **No written sponsorship policy** | If it's not in writing, it doesn't exist. Verbal promises are unenforceable. |
| **Repayment clauses for H-1B costs** | The DOL prohibits requiring employees to repay H-1B petition costs if they leave before a set date. This is treated as a prohibited penalty. |
| **High turnover of international employees** | May indicate the company uses sponsorship as a retention trap rather than a genuine benefit. |
| **"Benching" (no pay between projects)** | Illegal. Employers must pay H-1B workers the required wage for all nonproductive time caused by employer conditions, including lack of assigned work. Penalties range from $1,000 to $35,000 per violation. |
| **Staffing/body shop model** | Companies that place you at client sites and cycle through short contracts create instability. Check if the employer has a high ratio of entry-level (Level I) wage filings on H1B Grader — a telltale sign. |
| **Requires you to find your own project** | Legitimate employers assign work. Making you find your own client project shifts business risk onto you. |
| **No named immigration attorney** | Serious sponsors have a law firm on retainer. "We'll figure it out" is not a plan. |

## 5. Cost Breakdown: What Employers Actually Pay

Understanding fees helps you evaluate whether an employer is cutting corners.

### H-1B Petition Costs (2026)

| Fee | Amount | Who Pays |
|-----|--------|----------|
| Registration fee | $215 | Employer |
| Base filing fee (I-129) | $780 | Employer |
| ACWIA training fee | $750 (small) / $1,500 (large) | Employer |
| Fraud prevention fee | $500 | Employer |
| Asylum program fee | $600 | Employer |
| Premium processing (optional) | $2,965 | Employer (employee may pay only if for personal convenience) |
| Attorney fees | $2,000 - $5,000 | Employer |
| **Total (large employer, with premium)** | **~$8,560+** | |

### Green Card (PERM → I-140 → I-485)

| Step | Cost | Who Pays |
|------|------|----------|
| PERM labor certification + recruitment | $2,500 - $5,000+ | Employer |
| I-140 petition | $715 | Employer |
| Attorney fees (full GC process) | $5,000 - $15,000 | Employer must pay PERM; employee may pay I-140/I-485 |
| I-485 adjustment of status | $1,440 | Employee may pay |
| **Total green card process** | **$10,000 - $25,000+** | |

## 6. Negotiating Immigration Support in Your Offer

### When to Bring It Up

- **Do not** raise sponsorship in the first interview. Focus on demonstrating your value.
- **After receiving a written offer**, review it for immigration language. If missing, request it be added.
- If you are on OPT/STEM OPT, frame it as: "You already know my work. Here's the business case for continued investment."

### What to Negotiate

1. **H-1B filing timeline.** Request filing in the first available lottery cycle after your start date.
2. **Green card timeline.** Ask for PERM initiation within 12-18 months. Get it in writing.
3. **Premium processing.** Request the employer cover premium processing for both initial filing and any extensions.
4. **Dependent coverage.** Ask the employer to cover H-4/H-4 EAD filing costs for your spouse.
5. **Cap-gap protection.** If your OPT expires before H-1B starts (October 1), confirm the employer understands cap-gap extensions.

### How to Frame It

> "I want to be fully focused on delivering results, not worrying about immigration logistics. Can we include the sponsorship timeline in the offer letter so we're aligned?"

Build a document highlighting your achievements, unique expertise, and the cost of replacing you — employers are more receptive when they see sponsorship as retention investment, not charity.

## 7. What to Do If Your Employer Delays Sponsorship

### Immediate Steps

1. **Document everything.** Save your offer letter, any emails mentioning sponsorship, and records of verbal promises. Note dates and witnesses.
2. **Request a written timeline.** Ask your manager and HR for a specific PERM/H-1B filing date in writing.
3. **Set an internal deadline.** If the employer has not acted within 6-12 months of their stated timeline, escalate.

### If They Still Don't Act

| Option | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Transfer H-1B to a new employer** | Under H-1B portability, you can start working for a new employer as soon as a new I-129 petition is filed and a receipt is issued. You do not need to wait for approval. |
| **Self-petition (EB-1A or EB-2 NIW)** | If you have exceptional achievements, you can file your own green card petition without employer sponsorship. |
| **Consult an employment attorney** | If your offer letter promised sponsorship and the employer refuses, you may have a breach of contract claim. |
| **Confidential job search** | Use [H1B Grader](https://h1bgrader.com/), [MyVisaJobs](https://www.myvisajobs.com/), and the [USCIS Data Hub](https://www.uscis.gov/tools/reports-and-studies/h-1b-employer-data-hub) to identify sponsor-friendly employers. |

### Do Not

- Wait passively hoping things will change.
- Let your status lapse — know your grace periods (60-day grace period after H-1B termination).
- Accept "we'll do it next year" more than once.

## 8. Your Rights as a Sponsored Employee

### H-1B Portability

You are **not** trapped with your sponsoring employer. Under the American Competitiveness in the Twenty-First Century Act (AC21):

- **H-1B transfer:** You can change employers at any time. You may begin working for the new employer as soon as a nonfrivolous I-129 petition is filed and USCIS issues a receipt notice. You do not need approval first.
- **Eligibility:** You must be in valid H-1B status (or within the 60-day grace period after employment ends), and the new employer must have a certified LCA.

### Green Card Portability (AC21, Section 106)

If you are in the green card process and your I-485 (adjustment of status) has been **pending for 180+ days**:

- You can change employers **without losing your place in line**.
- Your I-140 approval remains valid even if your former employer withdraws it.
- The new job must be in the **same or similar occupational classification** as the one in your labor certification.
- Notify USCIS of the job change. Engage an immigration attorney experienced in AC21 portability.

### H-1B Extensions Beyond 6 Years (AC21, Section 104/106)

- If your PERM labor certification has been pending for **365+ days**, you can extend H-1B beyond the normal 6-year limit in 1-year increments.
- If you have an **approved I-140**, you can extend in 3-year increments.
- This prevents you from being forced to leave the country while waiting in green card backlogs.

### Wage and Working Condition Protections

| Right | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Prevailing wage guarantee** | Employer must pay at least the prevailing wage for your occupation and location, or the actual wage paid to similar employees — whichever is higher. |
| **No benching** | Employer must pay your full salary even during nonproductive periods (no project, office closure, etc.). Benching is illegal. |
| **No fee shifting** | Employer cannot require you to pay H-1B filing fees, attorney fees, or ACWIA/fraud fees. |
| **No penalty for quitting** | Employer cannot impose financial penalties for leaving before a specified date. Repayment clauses for H-1B costs are prohibited. |
| **Right to report violations** | You can file complaints with the [DOL Wage & Hour Division](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd) without retaliation. |

## 10. Quick Reference: The Sponsorship Checklist

Before you sign an offer, confirm the following:

- [ ] H-1B sponsorship is confirmed **in writing** in the offer letter
- [ ] Green card sponsorship timeline is stated (or at minimum, the company has a policy)
- [ ] Employer pays **all** mandatory H-1B fees (no cost-sharing)
- [ ] No repayment clauses for visa costs if you leave
- [ ] Company uses a **named immigration law firm**
- [ ] Check employer on USCIS Data Hub: approval rate **above 90%**
- [ ] Check employer on H1B Grader: grade **B or above**
- [ ] Employer has history of sponsoring employees (not just one-off filings)
- [ ] No benching risk: you will be assigned to a direct role, not placed at client sites
- [ ] Premium processing is available if needed
